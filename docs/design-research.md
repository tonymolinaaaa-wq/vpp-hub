# Cross-Category Design & Voice Research — Valley Painting Pros

> Working document feeding Section 08 (Aesthetic & Voice) of the Brand Brief v2.0. Cross-category precedents from premium service businesses, distilled into 11 build-ready design principles for `CLAUDE.md`.

**Bottom line up front:** The East Valley painting category is competing on a flat plane of "family-owned," "transform your space," and stock hero copy. To break out, valleypaintingpros.com should borrow conversion architecture from Mercury and Bench, gallery and editorial discipline from Bates Masi and Studio McGee, voice discipline from Patagonia and Mejuri, and atmosphere from Ace and The Hoxton — none of which any painter in Phoenix is doing.

---

## SECTION A — Painting-Category Anti-Patterns

Six patterns recur across the top 10 East Valley painting websites and the national painting category. Each is conspicuously absent from premium service categories. Design against all six.

### A1. The Vague-Superlative Hero
**Example:** Freedom Painting Professionals opens with "Welcome to Freedom Painting Professionals — your trusted, family-owned painting company serving Arizona's East Valley… Our commitment to exceptional craftsmanship, ethical practices, and customer satisfaction sets us apart as the premier choice." Arizona Painting Company leads with "Arizona Painting Company is your choice when you need expert painters with over 20 Years of experience."
**Why it fails:** Five adjectives, zero specifics. "Exceptional," "premier," "expert" are mathematically meaningless — the homeowner cannot rank one site against the next, which says the same thing. The hero burns its one chance to make a falsifiable claim.
**Better:** Lead with a number that can be measured against. VPP's hero should state the $300/day deadline credit, the daily SMS photo cadence, or "Ricardo runs every estimate himself" — claims a competitor cannot copy without proof.

### A2. "Family-Owned and Operated" as the Whole Differentiation Story
**Example:** Freedom Painting, Henry the Painter, McMill Painting, Coleman Painting, Brassberrys Painting — roughly 8 of the top 10 East Valley results lean on "family-owned" as their primary trust claim.
**Why it fails:** Every competitor says it, so the phrase signals nothing. Worse, it is the *banned* VPP voice pattern — it substitutes a category cliché for a real promise.
**Better:** Name the owner and put his face on it. The premium-service move is a *named human in first person* — "Ricardo Parra, owner, runs every estimate" — not "our family."

### A3. Trust Credentials Buried Below the Fold or in the Footer
**Example:** Freedom Painting lists ROC 321493 only on individual city pages, never in the hero. Most Angi/Yelp-listed painters cite license/insurance only on the "About" page or in a footer disclaimer.
**Why it fails:** The HOA-pressed homeowner is screening for legitimacy first; if she has to hunt for AZ ROC and insurance amounts, the page has already lost the trust test.
**Better:** ROC #363664 · Class CR-34 Dual · $1M/$2M insured · A+ BBB displayed as IBM Plex Mono metadata adjacent to the hero headline, not buried.

### A4. The Stock-Transform Photo + "Transform Your Space" Copy Pair
**Example:** McMullen's ("Transform Your Space with Professional Painting Services"); CertaPro ("transforms homes and businesses by providing expert services with a proven process"); Freedom Painting ("Don't wait any longer to give your home or business the fresh, vibrant look it deserves"). Most galleries use generic interior stock photography or low-resolution job-site phone snaps with no captions.
**Why it fails:** "Transform" is the banned VPP word. Stock photography is a confession that the company does not own its own work product visually. The gallery — the highest-converting asset on a service site — becomes a liability.
**Better:** Captioned, geo-tagged, neighborhood-named project tiles in the Studio McGee / Bates Masi mold. "Ocotillo two-story exterior · Dunn-Edwards DEC756 · 4 days on site · Aug 2025." Plain English, real metadata.

### A5. Auto-Rotating Testimonial Sliders / Carousel-Driven Social Proof
**Example:** Arizona Painting Company, Crash of Rhinos, Freedom Painting, and CertaPro all use auto-advancing carousels of social proof on home and service pages.
**Why it fails:** Carousels test poorly in named usability research. Nielsen Norman Group warns that auto-advancing content moves before users can read it. Baymard Institute's homepage-carousel benchmark concludes that 46% of desktop and mobile sites with a homepage carousel have implementations with usability issues. Auto-sliders also signal a generic web template rather than a custom build.
**Better:** Three to five static, named, neighborhood-tagged testimonials with first name + last initial + Chandler neighborhood. No motion. Same StoryBrand instinct as Patagonia's customer field reports: static, attributable, specific.

### A6. The Discount/Coupon-Driven Hero
**Example:** State 48 Painting Company opens every page with "Limited-Time Offer: Get 20% off any painting job." Valor Painting on Angi: "Take 15% off any project!" Crash of Rhinos: "no upfront payment, no deposits."
**Why it fails:** Discount-led positioning is the inverse of premium positioning. It tells the upper-middle-income homeowner that price is the only lever — which signals to her that quality probably is not the lever. Aesop, Mejuri, Mercury, Patagonia: none ever lead with a percentage off.
**Better:** Lead with the *guarantees* that have economic weight ($300/day completion credit, lifetime touch-up kit, 12-month wellness check). These read as confidence, not desperation.

## SECTION B — Cross-Category Precedents by Theme

Each theme covers at least three precedents from at least two categories. Every precedent ends with a concrete VPP translation.

### THEME 1 — Visual System (typography hierarchy, color discipline, restraint)

**Aesop · aesop.com.** Aesop uses Suisse Int'l paired with Bitstream's Zapf Humanist (Optima variant), a strict two-stroke-width system, and a monochromatic palette of muted yellows + black + gray on white. Headlines are restrained serif; product attributes use bold labels with regular values; everything aligns to a column grid. *Why it works:* The restraint is the brand signal — no decorative element competes with the product. *VPP translation:* Apply the two-weight discipline literally — Archivo Black for display headlines (one orange word), Inter Regular for body, Inter Medium only for attribute labels (e.g., "Color:" "Coats:" "Surface:"). No italics, no bold body emphasis. Every section uses the locked Bone → Ink → Bone → Putty alternation as Aesop uses its monochromatic background variations.

**Mercury · mercury.com.** Mercury commissioned its own typeface family (Arcadia), avoids the standard fintech blue/green for a desaturated palette with a single deep violet (#533afd) action color, and treats financial data as a first-class typographic element with its own weight and tracking. *Why it works:* Proprietary typography is a defensible brand moat; the single accent color makes every CTA legible without competing decorative color; treating numbers as their own typographic class makes financial data scannable. *VPP translation:* Treat metadata (ROC number, insurance amount, daily photo timestamps, paint product codes) as Mercury treats financial data — wide-tracked IBM Plex Mono ALL CAPS, slightly larger than body, never compressed into a sentence. Cap Signal Orange at ~10% per surface exactly as Mercury caps deep violet usage. CTAs are the only fully orange-filled elements on the page.

**Stripe · stripe.com.** Stripe uses a single font family (Sohne, with system-ui fallback), font-weights 300–500 (no high-contrast bold headlines), an 8px spacing scale, and decorative gradients only in hero sections — never in body. *Why it works:* Light weights at large sizes feel confident rather than shouty; the consistent 8px spacing scale is what makes the page feel "premium" before any visual element is consciously noticed. *VPP translation:* Adopt an 8px spacing token system in the build so every margin/padding is a multiple of 8. Reserve Archivo Black 900 exclusively for display headlines; do not use a bold weight of Inter anywhere in body copy.

**Bates Masi + Architects · batesmasi.com.** Full-bleed architectural photography, minimal chrome, and project tiles labeled with just project name + location + a single sentence. The firm displays its accolades as a hairline element ("Winner of 260+ Design Awards Since 2003"). *Why it works:* The work carries the page; copy stays out of the way. *VPP translation:* On the VPP gallery, every project tile gets a name (the neighborhood or street), a single Mid #5A4A3A metadata line ("Power Ranch · exterior repaint · 5 days"), and full-bleed photography. No card chrome, no rounded corners over photo, no captions overlaid on the image.

### THEME 2 — Voice and Tone (owner-accountability, plain English, named risk-reversal)

**Patagonia · patagonia.com.** Patagonia's product and editorial copy avoids adjectives in favor of plain matter-of-fact description. The "Don't Buy This Jacket" campaign worked because Patagonia had already published the Footprint Chronicles supply-chain data; the voice is earned, not declared. *Why it works:* Concrete nouns, no superlatives, public accountability data — readers trust what they can verify. *VPP translation:* Process pages should read like Patagonia's product descriptions: "We pressure-wash, scrape loose paint, sand transitions, prime bare stucco, caulk every cracked joint, two coats Dunn-Edwards Evershield, walk-through with you before final invoice." No "premium," no "meticulous," no "world-class."

**Mejuri · mejuri.com.** Per text-analytics research on best-seller product pages, Mejuri is "35.3x more likely to use the word handcrafted and infinitely more likely to talk about quality throughout product copy." Mejuri also names ethical sourcing, voices the founder in first person on About, and lists an "Estimated Retail Price" next to the "Mejuri Price" so the value claim is *shown*, not declared. *Why it works:* The brand earns premium positioning through craft-specific verbs and price transparency rather than adjectives. *VPP translation:* Every VPP service page should price-anchor with named numbers ("Cabinet refinish, average 14-cabinet kitchen: $4,200–$5,800 · 7 working days · Lifetime touch-up kit included"), not "affordable" or "competitive." Quote Ricardo in first person on About the way Mejuri quotes Noura Sakkijha — a signed pull-quote that names the founder and uses "I."

**Mercury · mercury.com.** Mercury's hero voice is sharp and declarative. The current homepage leads with the H1 "Radically different banking" and the subhead "Apply online in 10 minutes to experience banking unlike anything that's come before." Headlines are statements, never questions. FDIC disclosure appears as a transparent product fact lower on the page, not buried legalese. *Why it works:* Confident statements transfer confidence to the reader; questions hand framing back. *VPP translation:* Never phrase a VPP hero or section header as a question. "Painting. Done right." stays. Subheads become "We don't run multiple jobs at once," "The estimate has your address, our license number, and a date," "If we miss the date, you get $300 a day back."

**Shea McGee · mcgeeandco.com/pages/about-us.** Shea McGee opens McGee & Co.'s About in unmistakable first person: "While building our design firm, Studio McGee, I fell in love with the products that made each home look and feel beautiful… As a customer myself, I knew the pains of searching through hundreds (and hundreds) of options." EyeSwoon's About signs Athena Calderone's founder statement personally. *Why it works:* The "I" voice creates a person-to-person relationship and is the StoryBrand-aligned move that keeps the founder a *guide* (someone who solved her own version of the problem) rather than the hero. *VPP translation:* The locked VPP About story inverts the Shea McGee opener — instead of "I started Valley Painting Pros because…" in paragraph one, the locked story leads with the homeowner's fear ("Hiring a painter is harder than it should be") and reaches Ricardo's first-person voice at paragraph three. This is intentional and outperforms the founder-first opener for the HOA-pressed homeowner persona. Use the locked story (`/docs/about-story.md`) verbatim. Gereimy is named once in the locked story; if a separate Meet the Team page gets added later, she gets her own named block there.

**Hims · hims.com.** Hims uses plainspoken, slightly irreverent copy in a category that traditionally hides behind euphemism. The voice converts because it treats the reader as a peer, not a patient. *Why it works:* Plain English is the differentiator in a category trained on jargon. *VPP translation:* Treat "painting" the way Hims treats "telehealth" — drop the trade jargon. Replace "comprehensive surface preparation" with "we pressure-wash, scrape, sand, prime, caulk." Replace "premium coatings" with the actual paint product names and the warranty length.

### THEME 3 — Conversion Architecture (form design, trust signals, CTA logic, proof-before-pitch)

**Mercury · mercury.com.** The Mercury homepage leads with a confident H1 ("Radically different banking") and a single primary CTA ("Open account") backed by "Apply online in 10 minutes." The FDIC product fact ("up to $5M FDIC insurance") sits as a product feature card immediately below the hero. *Why it works:* One above-fold action, no carousel, no secondary CTA competing. The proof signal sits one scroll-band below the hero — close enough to land before the user navigates away, distinct enough not to clutter the headline. *VPP translation:* The VPP hero loads in this order: (1) the "Painting. Done right." wordmark, (2) the one-orange-word headline, (3) one IBM Plex Mono proof line — "AZ ROC #363664 · CLASS CR-34 DUAL · A+ BBB · $1M/$2M INSURED" — (4) the single primary CTA "Get your written estimate."

**Bench Accounting · bench.co/pricing.** Bench publishes named monthly tiers with public prices on a billing-cadence toggle (annual vs. monthly). Each tier carries a bulleted "what's included" list naming the human role at that level ("dedicated bookkeeping team," "dedicated tax professional"). *Why it works:* Public prices kill the price-shopper objection ("they must be expensive if they hide the number"); the named-human language at every tier makes the service feel personal at scale rather than commoditized. *VPP translation:* Each VPP service page publishes an honest price *range* with conditions ("Single-story stucco exterior, ~2,000 sqft, Dunn-Edwards Evershield: $4,800–$7,200; Ricardo gives the exact number after a 30-minute walk-through"). Every tier names the human accountable: "Ricardo on estimate, Gereimy on field ops, two crew members on site."

**Everlane · everlane.com/about.** Everlane invented the cost-breakdown product page: products list "Materials, Hardware, Labor, Duties, Transport → True Cost," then show the Everlane Price next to a "Traditional Retail" comparison. *Why it works:* The transparency *is* the conversion lever. The buyer cannot challenge the markup once she sees the math. *VPP translation:* On the "How we price" page, publish an Everlane-style breakdown for a representative exterior repaint: Paint product cost · Labor hours × rate · Prep materials · Equipment amortization · Insurance allocation · Overhead · Profit margin → Your price · vs. typical Valley pricing. This is the most powerful single page no East Valley painter has.

**Linear · linear.app.** Linear's marketing pages use a "gentle" interaction model — animations flow rather than punch, every component sits on the 8px scale, and CTAs use a single Inter sans on dark for instant recognizability. *Why it works:* Restraint signals competence; buttons feel earned because they don't shout. *VPP translation:* All VPP buttons share one fill (Signal Orange, Bone text) and one ghost variant (Ink outline on Bone). No gradient buttons, no shadow variants, no hover scale > 1.02. The estimate form's "Submit" button is the loudest single element above the fold and the only one filled in Signal Orange.

### THEME 4 — Gallery and Photography Treatment

**Studio McGee · studio-mcgee.com/projects.** Each Studio McGee project gets a project-tour treatment: full-bleed cover image, in-text labeled before/after pairs (not slider toys), a paragraph of context, and metadata. They turn one job into 12 blog posts, 26 long-form videos, and hundreds of social posts. *Why it works:* Each project carries story, not just imagery. The portfolio sells without pitching. *VPP translation:* Build the VPP gallery as project pages, not a flat grid. Every project page contains: cover (full-bleed), 4–6 before/afters stacked vertically with neighborhood name + paint product code, a short paragraph from Ricardo describing the curveball or detail on the job, and the daily SMS photos as a timeline strip at the bottom.

**Bates Masi + Architects · batesmasi.com.** Project tiles include a single-sentence design narrative and the firm names its accolades count in a hairline element ("Winner of 260+ Design Awards Since 2003"). *Why it works:* The single-sentence narrative gives a busy reader enough to care about the image; quantified accolades are more persuasive than vague claims of awards. *VPP translation:* Each VPP project tile carries one metadata sentence in Mid #5A4A3A: "Ocotillo · two-story stucco exterior · 4 days on site · Evershield in DEC756 Adobe Beige." No more, no less.

**Roman and Williams · romanandwilliams.com.** Their portfolio site is a slow vertical scroll of full-bleed hero photography with short, named context, treating each project like a chapter rather than a card in a deck. *Why it works:* Slow reading rewards the user with depth, which is exactly the feeling a premium service buyer wants. *VPP translation:* On the VPP project pages, photography is full-bleed at desktop (no margins on hero shots), captions live in the next vertical band on Bone background, never overlaid on the image. Mobile: photo bands edge-to-edge, captions stacked below.

**Mercury · mercury.com.** Mercury commissioned its own photography rather than licensing stock — cinematic landscapes with cool shadows and warm highlights, deliberate gradient overlays to land readable text. *Why it works:* Owned photography is uncopyable; stock immediately puts a brand at parity with competitors who use the same library. *VPP translation:* Commission a one-day shoot with a local Phoenix architectural photographer for 8–10 hero shots (Ricardo at a site, the crew taping, the lifetime touch-up kit on a clean surface, a homeowner shaking hands at the front door). Use these exclusively. Forbid stock paint cans, stock smiling homeowners, stock generic suburbs anywhere on the site.

### THEME 5 — Homepage / Hero Architecture

**Mercury · mercury.com.** Proof- and product-first opening: declarative H1, single CTA, single commissioned hero image, no carousel, no question. The product proof (FDIC, security, controls) appears as feature cards immediately below the hero. *Why it works:* The financial decision-maker is screening for legitimacy in the first second. *VPP translation:* The HOA-pressed homeowner and the kitchen refresher are both screening for legitimacy in the first second. Lead the hero with the statement headline and the Plex Mono license/insurance metadata line. Place the five Promises as feature cards in the very next band. Save story for the About page.

**Hims · hims.com.** Service-first opening with a plain-English subhead and a single bold CTA in a black filled box overlaid on a clean image. Two top-nav items ("Shop" and "Learn"), plus Cart and Login on the right. *Why it works:* Removing choices removes friction. *VPP translation:* Top nav holds at four items maximum: Services · Gallery · About · Get Estimate. No "Resources," no "Blog dropdown," no "Why Choose Us." The Get Estimate item is the only one styled as a filled orange pill.

**The Hoxton · thehoxton.com/about-us.** Story-first opening: a single positioning sentence ("The Hoxton is a series of open-house hotels inspired by the diversity and originality of the streets and scenes that surround us"), followed by city/neighborhood entry points and a values list. *Why it works:* For lifestyle-driven buyers, story is the wedge. The neighborhood is positioned as the brand's reason for being. *VPP translation:* For the fresh-start homeowner persona, the secondary scroll-band below the proof-led hero should open with a geographic statement — "We work in Chandler, Gilbert, Mesa, Queen Creek, Tempe, and Scottsdale. We don't subcontract. Ricardo personally visits every home before any estimate is written." Geographic specificity functions as story.

**Ace Hotel · acehotel.com.** Ace treats the hotel like a cultural object rather than a room inventory — atmosphere first, booking second. *Why it works:* Premium positioning sells the feeling, not the transaction. *VPP translation:* The VPP homepage closes (just above the footer) with an atmosphere band — a single full-bleed photograph of a finished East Valley home at golden hour, with one short paragraph titled "What we want for you" or similar, written in Ricardo's first-person voice. This is the one place the page allows feeling.

### THEME 6 — Service-Page Architecture

**Bench · bench.co/pricing.** Bench's service-tier pages structure as: headline statement → price (with annual/monthly toggle) → "What's included" bulleted list naming the human role → secondary FAQ. *Why it works:* The reader can resolve "what does it cost, who do I work with, what do I get" in one screen. *VPP translation:* Each VPP service page (Exterior Repaint, Interior Repaint, Cabinet Refinishing, HOA Champion Service) follows: headline → price range with conditions → named owner photo + "Ricardo runs every estimate" line → bulleted "What we do" in plain-English verbs → bulleted "What's included with every job" with the five Promise items → FAQ.

**Studio McGee.** Service pages (Full-Service Design, Virtual Design) include roughly 500 words of copy, illustrated step-by-step process, and exhaustive FAQs answered in McGee's voice. *Why it works:* Depth converts the considered-purchase reader; shallow pages send her to a competitor. *VPP translation:* Cabinet Refinishing — the longest decision cycle (2–8 weeks per the audience brief) — gets the deepest service page: full process walkthrough (degrease, sand, fill, prime, two coats, hardware reinstall, day-by-day timeline), color-system explainer, three before/after pairs from real Chandler/Gilbert jobs, FAQ covering the seven questions Ricardo answers most often. Target 800–1,200 words.

**Patagonia / Footprint Chronicles.** Product pages link directly to supply-chain detail for the specific item, allowing customers to trace journey from raw material to finished good. *Why it works:* The proof is one click from the pitch. *VPP translation:* Every VPP service page contains a link to the matching "How we price" breakdown page (Everlane-style), and to the matching project gallery filtered for that service. Internal cross-links function as continuous proof.

### THEME 7 — About / Founder Page (StoryBrand-aligned, founder-as-guide not hero)

**Shea McGee · mcgeeandco.com/pages/about-us.** First-person founder voice, named ("I"), concrete origin (the spare bedroom, the lack of room for fabric samples), then pivots to the customer's pain point. *Why it works:* The opening "I" creates intimacy; the pivot to "as a customer myself" makes the founder a fellow traveler, not a hero. *VPP translation:* The locked VPP About story (in `/docs/about-story.md`) inverts the Shea McGee opener — it leads with the homeowner's fear, not Ricardo's biography, and reaches first-person at paragraph three rather than paragraph one. This is deliberate and was chosen specifically for the HOA-pressed and kitchen-refresher personas, who screen for empathy before credential. Use the locked story verbatim; do not rearrange to fit the Shea McGee pattern. Gereimy is named once in the story; if a "Meet the Team" page is added later, Gereimy gets her own named block there.

**Athena Calderone · eye-swoon.com/pages/about.** Mixes third-person bio with a signed first-person founder pull-quote. *Why it works:* The signed quote feels personal and accountable; the third-person bio carries the credentials without sounding like the founder is bragging. *VPP translation:* The locked About story already does both register layers — the homeowner-fear opener carries the emotional weight, and Ricardo's first-person voice arriving at paragraph three carries the accountable credential. If a separate sidebar or Meet the Team page is added later, use a third-person credential block that draws facts directly from the brand brief in `/docs/brand-brief-v2.md` (the specific years, the trades he came up through, the AZ ROC and insurance details) — never invent or approximate numbers in design examples. Pair the credential block with a signed first-person pull-quote that articulates the why.

**Mercury · mercury.com/about.** Mercury's About uses concrete origin language and names the tea-serving ritual as a small specific cultural artifact. *Why it works:* Specific small artifacts feel real; vague mission statements do not. *VPP translation:* Find Ricardo's small artifact — the lifetime touch-up kit, the printed estimate handed over in person, the four daily SMS photos — and write one paragraph about *why* he started doing it, the way Mercury writes about the tea. This is the moment the homeowner decides VPP is different.

**Patagonia · patagonia.com/activism.** Yvon Chouinard's voice and accountability practice show up in the About / activism architecture, where the founder's stated commitments are paired with verifiable data. *Why it works:* The founder is a guide whose conviction is documented, not declared. *VPP translation:* On the About page, name three commitments Ricardo verifies publicly — the $300/day credit (with the rule clearly written), the 12-month wellness check (with the standard scope), and the lifetime touch-up kit (what it contains, how it's labeled). Three commitments documented are worth ten adjectives.

## SECTION C — 11 Design Principles for CLAUDE.md

Extraction-ready. Each principle is stated as a do/don't, anchored in a named cross-category precedent, and translated into a specific build behavior.

1. **Headlines are statements, never questions.** *(Source: Mercury's "Radically different banking" H1, Linear's declarative product copy, Patagonia's matter-of-fact product descriptions.)* Every H1 and section H2 is a declarative sentence ending with a period. The hero is "Painting. Done right." not "Looking for a painter you can trust?" Subheads such as "We don't run multiple jobs at once" and "The estimate has your address, our license number, and a date" follow the same rule.

2. **Proof signals appear above the fold as primary metadata, not as footer disclosure.** *(Source: Mercury's product-feature card placement of FDIC coverage; Bench's named human roles per pricing tier.)* On every page, an IBM Plex Mono ALL-CAPS metadata line displays AZ ROC #363664 · CLASS CR-34 DUAL · A+ BBB · $1M/$2M INSURED. This line sits between the wordmark and the H1, never in the footer.

3. **Body copy never uses bold for emphasis; eyebrow-then-headline rhythm carries emphasis instead.** *(Source: Aesop's two-stroke-width system; Mejuri's craft-verb-driven copy; Stripe's font-weight-300/400 hierarchy.)* No `<strong>` inside paragraph copy. Emphasis is achieved by an IBM Plex Mono eyebrow line above the Archivo Black headline. Inter Medium is reserved for attribute *labels* in spec lists, never sentence emphasis.

4. **Photography is owned, not licensed.** *(Source: Mercury's commissioned cinematic landscape program; Studio McGee's owned project tours; Roman and Williams' full-bleed in-house photography.)* No stock paint cans, no stock smiling homeowners, no generic suburb hero photos. All site photography comes from a commissioned shoot of real VPP projects, Ricardo on site, the crew at work, and the lifetime touch-up kit. Each photo's file name encodes neighborhood + service + date.

5. **The Signal Orange cap is enforced at the component level.** *(Source: Mercury's deep-violet single-accent discipline; Aesop's monochromatic restraint.)* No section's total orange ink (text, fills, accents) exceeds ~10% of the surface. Filled-orange CTAs are the only fully-saturated orange elements per section. Headlines contain exactly one orange word. Diagrams and dividers use Rule #D9D2C0, not orange.

6. **Every service page publishes a named price range with conditions, plus an Everlane-style cost breakdown link.** *(Source: Bench's public-tier pricing; Everlane's True Cost line items; Mejuri's "Estimated Retail" vs. "Mejuri Price" comparison.)* No "affordable," no "competitive," no "request a quote for pricing." Each service page shows an honest range with the qualifying conditions in Plex Mono metadata, and links to a "How we price" page that breaks down a representative project line by line.

7. **The named human appears on every estimate-related surface.** *(Source: McGee & Co.'s first-person Shea McGee opener; EyeSwoon's signed Athena Calderone founder quote; Mercury's named co-founder voice; Hims's plain-English peer-to-peer copy.)* Ricardo's name and photo appear on the home hero proof block, every service page, the About page, and the estimate confirmation. Gereimy gets her own named block on About and on field-ops-related copy. We never write "our team" or "our experts" where a named human is the truthful substitute.

8. **Galleries are project pages with metadata, not card grids with lightboxes; no auto-rotating sliders anywhere.** *(Source: Studio McGee project tours; Bates Masi's single-sentence project-tile narrative; Roman and Williams's vertical-scroll chapter structure. Carousel research: Nielsen Norman Group; Baymard's finding that 46% of homepage carousels have usability issues.)* Each project gets its own URL with: full-bleed cover photo, neighborhood + service + duration + paint product code metadata line in Mid #5A4A3A, 4–6 stacked before/after pairs, a 60–120 word note from Ricardo, and a four-photo SMS timeline. No auto-rotating sliders for galleries, testimonials, or hero images.

9. **Testimonials are static, named, and neighborhood-tagged.** *(Source: Patagonia's static field-report customer narratives; Mejuri's named, sourced craft stories.)* Three to five static testimonials per page, each with first name + last initial + neighborhood ("Sarah K. · Power Ranch · exterior repaint, May 2025"). No carousels, no auto-advance, no anonymous five-star quotes.

10. **About uses the locked story verbatim; services close in named accountability.** *(Source: the locked About story in `/docs/about-story.md` was developed specifically for VPP and supersedes generic founder-page precedents for this project. The story leads with the homeowner's fear before any Ricardo credential — a deliberate StoryBrand-aligned inversion of the Shea McGee / EyeSwoon "I"-opener pattern, designed to outperform the conventional founder-first opener for the HOA-pressed and kitchen-refresher personas. Yvon Chouinard / Patagonia's documented founder commitments paired with verifiable data provide the closing-section model.)* The About section uses the ~480-word locked story as-is. The opening paragraph names the homeowner's fear, not Ricardo. Ricardo's first-person voice arrives in paragraph three — never rearrange this. Treat the three quoted insider lines (one coat / power-washing / just finish it) as pull-quotes: indented, italic. The story closes with the five Promises and the tagline as sign-off. Every service page closes with a one-line accountability statement signed by Ricardo: "If we miss the date, you get $300 a day back. — Ricardo Parra, owner."

11. **The 8px spacing scale is the only spacing system, and section backgrounds alternate per the locked sequence.** *(Source: Stripe's documented 8px elementGap and spacing tokens; Linear's 8px-spacing-scale design system.)* All margins and padding are multiples of 8px. Section verticals use 64/96/128. Type scale uses defined steps; no one-off pixel values. Background alternation across sections follows Bone → Ink → Bone → Putty → Bone → Ink — never two adjacent sections share a background, never a fifth background color is introduced.

## Closing note

The category opportunity is large and currently empty. Across the top 10 East Valley painter results, no site combines named-owner first-person voice, public price transparency, owned project photography, and proof-first hero architecture. Each of these is table stakes in premium service categories outside painting. Build the four moves above, hold the Signal Orange discipline and the Plex Mono metadata system, and valleypaintingpros.com will read as the only premium painting business in the Valley.

## CAVEATS
